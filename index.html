Jean Genet

"C'est parce qu'elle n'est pas achevée qu'une action est infâme."

Genet was a French novelist, playwright, poet, essayist, and political activist.

Born in 1910 from a prostitute mother, he was placed for adoption and grew up in the provincial town of Alligny-en-Morvan, in the Nièvre department of central France.

His foster family was a caring one, and he received good grades in school, but he was involved in petty theft and attempts of running away, wich led to him being sent to a correctional facility from his 15 to 18.

He joined the Foreign Legion but was given a dishonorable discharge for indecency, having been caught engaged in a homosexual act.

He spent a period as a vagabond, petty thief and prostitute across Europe, wich you can read about in Le Journal du Voleur (1949).

He eventualy returned to Paris where he went in and out of prison for various offences. In prison he wrote his first poem (Le condamné à mort), and his first novel (Notre Dame des Fleurs, 1944).

He then met Jean Cocteau who was impressed by his writing. This connection allowed him to be published, and later saved him from a life sentence, thanks to a petition including among others Cocteau, Sartre and Picasso, convincing the French President to have the sentence set aside. Genet would never return to prison.

His writings were controversial, because they often included very explicit and provocative portrayals of homosexuality and criminality.

He became politicaly active around the late 1960s. He participated in demonstrations drawing attention to the living conditions of immigrants in France.

In 1970 he was invited by the Black Panthers and stayed three months with them, giving lectures, attenting trials and publishing articles in their journals. He later that year spent six months in Palestinian refugee camps, secretly meeting Yasser Arafat. Profoundly moved by those experiences, he wrote his final memoir, Un Captif Amoureux, wich would be published after his death.

Genet also supported Angela Davis and George Jackson. He worked with Foucault and Sartre to protest police brutality against Algerians in Paris.

In 1982, he was in Beirut when Sabra and Shatila massacre occured. Having visited Shatila after the event, he published Quatre heures à Chatila.

On 15 April 1986, Genet was found dead in his hotel room, in Paris. He had developed a throat cancer and he is believed to have fallen on the floor, fatally hitting his head. He is now resting in the Larache Christian Cemetery, in Morocco.
